# WARM WHEELERS — Design Brief

**Red Bull Soapbox Race · Johannesburg**

*An ordinary gas heater, racing down a hill — a tribute to the gas that got Joburg through winter.*

Date: 2026-06-21 · Status: Approved

---

## Theme

A soapbox cart built to be **instantly recognisable as an everyday indoor LPG gas heater** — modelled on the familiar Goldair-style radiant heater: dark matte body, front grille, control knobs, castor wheels, subtle nameplate. The driver wears an **orange LPG gas-bottle costume**, so seated, *heater + bottle = one complete, working-looking unit — the driver literally is the fuel.* The signature visual: **red panels behind the grille that the driver manually "ignites,"** lighting up bar by bar as the cart rolls.

The tone is **cheeky and tongue-in-cheek** — the absurd dignity of racing a household appliance — over a warm, heartfelt core: a build that races *out of* a brutal Highveld winter, paying **homage to the gas suppliers and deliveries that kept Joburg warm.** Branding stays a quiet "WARM WHEELERS" nameplate (right where the appliance brand badge sits); no third-party logos unless Red Bull approves.

## Driver story

The driver (to be cast) is imagined as a **lifelong F1 superfan and first-time racer** — the everyman who watches racing from the couch, finally getting a seat of his own… in a heater. His crew are a **gas-delivery team in overalls** who "deliver warmth." Simple, legible, no fiction required: *warmth, delivered.*

## Engineering goals

*In priority order:*

1. **Finish clean, safe and upright — and unmistakably a heater.**
2. **Genuinely fast, with good handling** on the clock.
3. **Reliable, driver-triggered glow** on every run.

Guiding principle: keep the **tall heater silhouette honest, but carry all mass low** (driver, bottle and ballast at the floor) for a low centre of gravity; smooth the airflow where it won't break the look. Deliberately **compact — well within Red Bull's 2 m × 2.5 m × 6 m maximum** for handling, buildability and cost. Working **brakes, steering, restraint and helmet**; built to **survive the course jump**, with driver visibility planned around the shell and costume.

Crucially, the design is **proven before it's built — CAD → FEA → CFD → validation → design freeze — so the structure is solid before a single piece is cut.**

## Crowd hook

*The 30-second start:*

1. Cart arrives looking like a **cold, dead heater** — dark panels.
2. The **gas-delivery crew** rush in and "connect" the **orange bottle-driver** to the heater.
3. Driver hits the trigger → **red panels flare to life, one by one** → *ignition.*
4. Crew signal "**warmth delivered**" and shove it off down the hill.

Purely visual — **no smoke, sound props or thrown items.** One clean, readable gag: *the driver is the fuel, and winter just ended.*

## First-time-builder limits

*The honest part.* One **handy builder, solo for now**, recruiting teammates and race-day pushers as the project grows. **Near-zero tooling** to start — part of the budget buys tools. **Self-funded at ~R3,000/month across June–August 2026 (~R9,000)** covering tools *and* materials, so the build stays **cost-disciplined, compact, and built from cheap, accessible materials.**

Deliberately **not attempting** custom suspension, hydraulics or fancy electronics — the aim is *modern, effective, well-engineered, nothing fancy.* The honest risk: a first build that has to be **structurally solid** — mitigated by the simulate-before-you-cut process above.
